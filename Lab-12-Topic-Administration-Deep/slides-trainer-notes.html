Section 3Topic Anatomy

06Section header

Topic Anatomy

Divider

Say

"Foundation first — what a topic actually IS. Ten minutes on this, then we go deep on sizing."

07Concept

A topic is a named, append-only stream

Big highlighted orders.events

Say

"Applications know the name. Kafka stores the data as partitions. The topic name is just a label the cluster maps to physical storage."

"Append-only means you write, never modify. That constraint is what makes replication, compaction, and offset tracking possible."

→ Partitions (the actual storage)
08Partitions

A topic is divided into partitions

3 partition boxes + 3-bullet properties + big takeaway

Say

"Each partition is an independent ordered log. Different partitions can live on different brokers."

"Point at the boxes: partitions 0, 1, 2 — each has its OWN offset sequence starting at 0. Offset 5 in P0 is a totally different record than offset 5 in P1."

Watch

Common confusion: students think "offset 100" is a global position in the topic. It's not — offsets are per-partition.

→ Offsets in detail
09Offsets

An offset is a position inside one partition

Notation slide: orders.events-2 @ 1057

Say

"This notation — topic-partition@offset — is standard. If your monitoring or logs use it, you know exactly which record they're pointing to."

"Monotonically increasing means the next record always gets offset+1. Even if that record is deleted later, the offset itself is never reused."

Watch

"Deleted records can create unavailable historical ranges" — meaning after retention deletes segment 0-999, offsets 0-999 return "out of range" but offsets 1000+ still work. Consumers must handle this.

→ Record structure
10Records

What a record carries

Two cards: payload vs positioning

Say

"Left card = things YOU set (payload). Right card = things Kafka assigns (positioning)."

"Headers are a KEY-VALUE map — often overlooked. Great for trace-ids, tenant tags, retry counts. Not part of the payload, not part of the key."

→ Ordering guarantees
11Ordering

Ordering guarantee

Green ✓ card + Red ✕ card + Design rule callout

Say

"The single most important sentence in Kafka: Kafka guarantees ordering within a partition, never across partitions."

"For our scenario: to keep events for one order ordered, they MUST all land in the same partition. That's what the key does."

Poll

"If I don't set a key, will my events be ordered?" (Trainees say yes / no with equal split. Answer: within a partition, yes. Across partitions, no. So without a key, events for order X may split across partitions and lose their relative order.)

→ How the partitioner picks a partition
12Partitioner

How a producer picks a partition

3-step numbered flow + formula + admin implication callout

Say

"Three cases in order of priority: explicit → key → default. Most apps hit case 2 (key)."

"Draw attention to the formula: hash(key) mod partitionCount. This is the piece that will bite us later when we grow partitions."

Watch

The "sticky" partitioner (case 3, no key) BATCHES records to one partition for throughput. It's not round-robin per record.

→ Hot partition problem
13Hot partition

The hot partition problem

Visual: P0 = 1k/s, P1 = 50k/s hot, P2 = 1k/s + causes + symptoms + danger callout

Say

"Point at the red P1 box. This is what a hot partition LOOKS like — 50x the other partitions."

"Causes are almost always the key strategy — either low cardinality or one whale customer."

Poll

"If I have a hot partition, will adding more partitions help?" (Trainees usually say yes. Answer: no, unless it fixes the key distribution.)

Watch

Five dimensions of the decision

→ Inputs to measure first
16Inputs

Measure first, calculate second

Table of 8 inputs with example values

Say

"You can't do the math without these numbers. If a workload team asks 'how many partitions?' and can't tell you their peak byte rate, send them back to measure first."

→ First formula: write side
17Formula

P_write formula

Big centered formula (peak ingress / safe MB/s per partition)

Say

"Peak ingress on top. Safe write per partition on the bottom. Result is partitions needed for the WRITE side alone."

"'Safe' means the rate at which the broker responds within your latency SLO, replication is stable, no ISR flapping. Not the burst rate that eventually kills things."

→ Example applied
18Example · write

P_write worked example

120 ÷ 15 = 8 partitions

Say

"Given box on top. Formula in the middle. Result: 8 partitions."

"The 15 MB/s number came from a real benchmark on OUR hardware. Not from a blog post."

Watch

Trainees ask "what if we don't have benchmark data?" Answer: use the kafka-producer-perf-test.sh tool on your cluster. See Sc 6.2 (quotas lab) for a working example, or dedicate an hour to it. Real numbers >> blog numbers.

→ Read side formula
19Formula

P_read formula

Big formula (group throughput / safe MB/s per consumer instance)

Say

"Same shape, but now we care about the CONSUMER'S bottleneck."

"Take the group with the WORST per-instance throughput — usually fraud detection, ML models, or anything with external calls."

→ Example
20Example · read

P_read worked example

120 ÷ 10 = 12 partitions

Say

"Fraud detection can only process 10 MB/s per instance. Need to process 120 MB/s total. Therefore 12 consumers, therefore at least 12 partitions."

→ Take the max
21The rule

Take the maximum

max(P_write, P_read, concurrency) = 12

Say

"Write side wanted 8. Read side wanted 12. We take 12 — it satisfies both."

"The 'concurrency' term catches the case where you want to run N consumers per group for redundancy, even if throughput doesn't require it."

→ Consumer parallelism rule
22Parallelism

Consumers per group ≤ partitions

Table showing 4 / 12 / 16 consumers on 12 partitions

Say

"Third row is the punchline: 16 consumers on 12 partitions = 4 idle consumers."

"You can't have more active consumers in a group than there are partitions. Extra consumers sit idle waiting for a rebalance."

Watch

The callout is important: multiple CONSUMER GROUPS don't share this limit. Group A can have 12 consumers reading, Group B another 12, independently.

→ How multiple groups multiply the READ load
23Multiple groups

Read load × groups

120 MB/s × 4 groups = 480 MB/s of reads

Say

"Ingress is 120 MB/s. But four consumer groups each read the whole thing. Cluster sees 480 MB/s of reads on top of the writes."

"Partition count decision must consider broker network AND disk read capacity — not just ingress."

→ Record rate vs byte rate
24Rate types

Record rate vs byte rate

Two workloads: 100k×1KB vs 1k×100KB — same MB/s, different pressure

Say

"Both workloads are 100 MB/s. But they stress Kafka completely differently."

"High record rate = per-record overhead dominates. Large records = memory and fetch-size pressure."

"Always benchmark with your ACTUAL record shape."

→ Compression affects the math
25Compression

Compression changes MB/s

200 MB/s / 2.5 = 80 MB/s + 6 factors that affect it

Say

"Compression looks like a free win but ratio depends on your data. Uniform JSON compresses great. Already-compressed images don't compress at all."

"Test with realistic data. The blog promises 4:1 with zstd; your actual traffic might get 1.5:1."

→ Replication also multiplies
26RF multiplier

Replication multiplies internal traffic

Ingress × RF ≈ cluster write traffic (120 × 3 = 360 MB/s)

Say

"Every byte you produce becomes RF bytes on the internal replication network."

"With RF=3, cluster sees 3x the ingress in wire traffic. Plus consumer fetch traffic on top. This is what your NIC actually carries."

→ Broker distribution check
27Distribution

Does the count fit the cluster?

12 partitions × RF 3 on 6 brokers = 6 replicas/broker, 2 leaders/broker

Say

"Simple arithmetic sanity check — did I pick a partition count that DIVIDES nicely across brokers?"

"But note the callout: this is COUNT balance. Real balance requires byte size, traffic, and rack placement checks too."

→ Storage sizing
28Storage

How much disk will this cost?

Primary = ingress × retention; Replicated = primary × RF (12.1 TB → 36.3 TB)

Say

"Two formulas stacked. Primary = one copy. Replicated = ×RF."

"36 TB isn't the disk you buy — it's before you add operational headroom, index files, protocol overhead, and 30% safety."

→ Per-partition size (recovery constraint)
29Per-partition size

Average partition size matters

Table: 6 vs 12 vs 24 partitions with resulting size

Say

"Same 12 TB, different partition counts. 24 partitions = 500 GB each. 6 partitions = 2 TB each."

"Big partitions are slow to recover after a failure. Small partitions multiply file handles."

→ Recovery time constraint
30Recovery

Recovery-time constraint

500 GB / 50 MB/s = 2.8 h

Say

"When a broker fails and comes back, the failed replica must be re-fetched from the leader. This is your Recovery Time."

"2.8 hours is theoretical. Real recovery is slower because production traffic + multiple replicas share the same disk and NIC."

Poll

"If your recovery objective is 30 minutes, is 500 GB per partition acceptable?" (Answer: no. Either shrink partitions or accept longer recovery.)

31Growth

Growth headroom

12 × 1.4 = 16.8 → round to 18

Say

"Add expected growth. 40% annual = plan for 40% more capacity than today."

"Warning callout is important: don't over-partition 'just in case'. Every partition has a cost — files, memory, controller work."

Replication & Durability

Divider — "Leader · Followers · ISR · min.ISR · acks"

Say

39RF concept

Replication factor = number of copies

3 brokers with leader/follower pills

Say

"3 partitions × RF 3 = 9 replicas total. Each partition has one leader (accepts writes) and RF-1 followers (copy from the leader)."

"Point at the visual — every broker has some leader replicas (blue) and some follower replicas (light blue). Leaders are spread across brokers for load balancing."

→ ISR
40ISR

In-Sync Replicas — who's caught up

Healthy vs degraded cards + reasons ISR shrinks

Say

"ISR = subset of replicas that are up-to-date with the leader. Only ISR members are eligible to become the new leader if the current leader fails."

"Read the 'why ISR shrinks' list — every one of these is a real production incident."

Watch

"GC pause" is the sneaky one. A broker in a long GC looks dead to the leader, gets kicked from ISR, then rejoins when GC completes. Chronic pattern → investigate heap tuning.

→ Durability triangle
41Durability

Three knobs, one guarantee

Three info cards: RF · min.ISR · acks + danger callout

Say

"THREE knobs. Any one weakened and the guarantee is broken."

"RF sets how many copies exist. min.ISR sets how many must actively confirm a write. acks tells the producer to wait for that confirmation."

"Repeat: min.ISR only matters when producer uses acks=all. Without acks=all, min.ISR is decoration."

→ The safety formula
42Formula

The safety formula

RF ≥ min.ISR + F · table of RF/min.ISR/F combinations

Say

"F = how many broker failures you tolerate without producer errors."

"Table gives three RF=3 setups. The middle row (min.ISR=2) is the industry recommendation."

"Top row (min.ISR=1) means you accept writes even when there's only ONE replica alive — silent data loss risk. Bottom row (min.ISR=3) means first ISR shrink fails writes — brittle."

Poll

"Which is safer — min.ISR=2 or min.ISR=3?" (Most say min.ISR=3, because "more required = more durable". Answer: min.ISR=3 is more BRITTLE — the first slow follower kills the producer. min.ISR=2 balances durability and availability.)

→ What happens on failure
43Failure

What happens when broker 1 dies

Broker visual with failed leader + failover sequence

Say

"Broker 1 dies. Controller detects. Elects a follower from the ISR as new leader. Clients refresh metadata. Total client impact: seconds to tens of seconds."

"Bottom callout is the key insight: recovery quality = replication health BEFORE the failure. If ISR was already shrunk, you have fewer options."

→ Unclean election trade-off
44Unclean election

Availability vs durability trade-off

Two comparison cards: unclean=false vs unclean=true

Say

"When ISR is completely empty, Kafka faces a choice."

"False (default since 0.11) = wait for an ISR replica. Partition stays offline. Zero data loss. Availability < durability."

"True = elect any surviving replica. Partition comes back. Data written but not yet replicated is silently lost."

Watch

Ancient clusters (pre-0.11) had default=true. Upgrades don't auto-fix. If your cluster is old, verify.

Section 7Retention, Compaction & Storage

53Section header

Retention, Compaction & Storage

Divider

Say

"How data leaves your topic. Two policies, several knobs each."

54Policies

Compare the two cleanup policies

Three cards: delete · compact · compact+delete

Say

"Delete = time / size based, removes old segments wholesale. Event history use case."

"Compact = keeps latest value per key. Current-state use case. REQUIRES keys."

→ Time-based retention
55Time retention

Retention operates on segments

retention.ms=604800000 + red trap callout

Say

"7 days in milliseconds. Every retention config uses ms."

"RED TRAP: retention deletes whole SEGMENTS, not individual messages. The active segment is never eligible. Data can outlive retention.ms by up to segment.ms."

Poll

"If I set retention.ms=1000 (1 second), does data get deleted after 1 second?" (Most say yes. Answer: only rolled segments older than 1s. Active segment persists indefinitely.)

→ Size-based retention
56Size retention

Per-partition, not per-topic

Formula: retention.bytes × P × RF

Say

"retention.bytes limits PER PARTITION. For a 6-partition RF=3 topic with 1 GB retention.bytes, actual footprint is ~18 GB."

"Warning callout: high-volume periods shorten the effective time window. Use time OR size — reasoning about both is hard."
